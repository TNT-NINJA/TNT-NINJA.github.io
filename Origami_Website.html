Origami

What is origami? Origami is traditionaly the art of folding a square of paper into various shapes and animals. These can range from simple cranes all the way to incredibly complex insects.

Some people may only think of origami as folding paper into animals, but there are many different branches of origami, such as creating geometrical shapes and figures like this.

The origins of origami aren't known for certain, but most people believe that origami originated in Japan. Early on, paper was expensive to make so origami was largely restricted to the upper-class and for ceremonies. In the 1600s, the price of producing paper was greatly reduced, which caused origami to spread to the rest of the population. In 1853, Japan's policy of commercial and cultural isolation from of the rest of the world ended. This opened origami to the rest of the world where instructional books for children on basic folds spread it to across the globe.

Origami isn't just an art form, it also has practical applications. Many of the techniques used in origami can also be applied to engineering problems.

  • For example, engineers have used origami origami principles to create a solar panel that folds up to a small size, but can be unfolded to a very large size. They are hoping to use this model in space where the solar panels can be sent up in a rocket compacted, and then expand with little astronaut assistance.   • Origami has also been used in marine biology. Robert Lang from Harvard University developed an origami inspired machine that can function under water. Its purpose is to collect soft bodied underwater creatures such as sponges and seaslugs without harming them. The machine is fully functional underwater, can easily capture underwater creatures, and can be made with a 3-d printer in mere hours.
  • Origami has also been used in the design of airbags in cars. Concepts from traditional origami have been used in order fold air bags into a small space in a way where they can both expand very rapidly and once expanded be strong enough to safely cushion a person in the event of a crash.
  • The field of medicine has also been improved by origami. A PhD student at MIT named Katerina Mantzavinou has worked on an application of origami to possibly treat cancer. it works through a capsule that carries drugs to a cancererous tumor and folds up small. Once it has reached the desired target it unfurls and realeases the drug to fight the tumor. The design hasn't been finished yet and she is is still working on making it small enough to reach the desired destination.
